## 2. Operating context (why “mining health” is not “hospital IT”)

At a large underground operation, health work spans:

- Multiple **clinical touchpoints** (clinics, posts, ED, follow-up)  
- **Rotating staff** and **contractor** populations  
- **Occupational** programmes (fitness for duty, testing, incidents)  
- **Pre-hospital** and **ambulance** pathways  
- **Inventory** and **equipment** that directly affects patient safety  
- **Regulatory and confidentiality** expectations that match or exceed general healthcare  

When the **information layer** does not reflect that reality, clinical teams compensate with heroics, paper, and informal communication. That is neither scalable nor defensible under scrutiny.

### 2.1 National agenda and global standard — don’t fall behind

**Direction of travel**  
Ghana’s health sector—like serious systems worldwide—is moving toward **digitizing** care and **centralizing** health information so that **the record can follow the patient** (or worker) **wherever** they legitimately access care. Continuity, security, and auditability are **mainstream expectations**, not optional extras.

**Implication for mining**  
A large, regulated operation that still depends on **paper charts**, **partial Excel**, and **scattered files** is **out of step** with that direction. It creates **unnecessary risk**: privacy gaps, weak M&E, duplicated tests, and difficulty demonstrating compliance when authorities or partners ask hard questions.

**Message for leadership**  
MineAid is not “technology for fashion”—it is **alignment** with where **national policy** and **global good practice** are already pointing: **one governed health information layer**, with **role-based access**, so care and occupational health can be **coordinated** across clinics, posts, and the hospital—not **reconstructed from binders** each time.

## 3. Gap analysis — what the current pattern produces

### 3.1 OHSE balance: health under-represented in the system landscape

**Observation**  
Occupational health, safety, and environment (**OHSE**) is the foundation of responsible mining and is treated with utmost seriousness. In practice, **safety systems and tooling often dominate** the digital and procedural landscape, while **health** can appear **folded into** safety workflows rather than served by a **dedicated, end-to-end health information management (HIM)** capability.

**Effect**  
There is **limited organisational emphasis** on hiring or empowering **health information managers** in the same way other critical mining disciplines are staffed. **Health data and management** on site can end up **“shoved in somewhere”**—paper files, shared drives, or spreadsheets—instead of a **primary system of record** for clinical and occupational health.

**Risk**  
The mine cannot fully answer: *Where is the authoritative health record for this worker, this incident, this programme, this handover—across time, site, and role?*

---

### 3.2 Paper + partial Excel: skeletal data, critical detail left behind

**Observation (multi-year pattern)**  
Clinics have relied on **paper-based health records**, with **only partial, skeletal information** captured in **Excel**—**without** the **critical clinical detail** needed for continuity, audit, and decision quality.

**Effects**

- **No single reference point** for a patient’s story across encounters.  
- **Confidentiality weak**: physical records can be **accessible to non-clinical staff** in ways that would not pass a disciplined privacy review.  
- **Regulatory exposure**: occupational and clinical programmes require **traceability**; paper + ad hoc spreadsheets struggle to demonstrate **who saw what, when, and why**.

---

### 3.3 Fragmentation across people, place, and programme

**Observation**  
With **rotations across multiple clinics** (e.g. five separate clinics referenced in your experience), the same patient may present **inconsistent or misleading information**—sometimes to obtain medications or obscure history. Without a **shared, authoritative record**, each clinician starts partly blind.

**Additional fracture points**

- **Movement** between departments or locations **without** portable, accessible health data.  
- **Duplication** of data: physically (files in different places) and technically (separate lists for medical care, testing, incidents, ambulance, SOPs—not reliably available to medical staff in one workflow).

**Risk**  
**Duplicated tests**, **conflicting prescriptions**, **delayed care**, and **unreliable reporting** for management and regulators.

---

### 3.4 Oversight, M&E, and standard of care

**Observation**  
As **Emergency Response Supervisor**, you are **stationed at one post** and **cannot be everywhere**. When operations depend on informal updates, **oversight** and **monitoring & evaluation** suffer.

**Effects**

- **Variable standards of care**: different clinicians approach similar cases differently when **protocols and records** are not embedded in a common system.  
- **Work “done but not documented”**: ambulance checks, equipment checks, and operational tasks may occur but **leave no durable, queryable trail**.  
- **Handover gaps**: a report to one supervisor may **stall** when both parties rotate off—**no central place** where the issue lives until closure.

**Risk**  
Incidents and near-misses are harder to learn from; quality improvement cycles lack **data**.

---

### 3.5 Medical inventory: paper checklists and supply risk

**Observation**  
Inventory has depended on **tedious paper checklists**.

**Effects**

- **Over- and under-stock**, **shortages**, **wastage** through **expiry**.  
- Weak link between **consumption**, **patient encounters**, and **forecasting**.

**Risk**  
Patient care and emergency readiness **directly impacted** by stock failure.

## 5. How MineAid HMS maps to these gaps (product alignment — not a feature dump)

MineAid is conceived as a **mining-focused, multi-tenant health management system**: clinical workflows, occupational programmes, incidents, testing, inventory, ambulance/EMS-style operations, governance (e.g. SOP library), and administration—with **tenant isolation** and **RBAC** designed for serious operations.

**Direct mappings (examples)**

- **Paper + Excel → structured records** for patients, visits, and programmes, with **auditability**.  
- **Scattered clinics → one tenant model** with role-appropriate access (clinical vs safety vs admin).  
- **Ambulance / equipment checks → documented, traceable** workflows instead of “done but invisible.”  
- **Inventory → digital movements, alerts, history** aligned to real site supply practice.  
- **Supervision → dashboards and reports** that do not require the supervisor to be physically present at every site to see patterns.

## 7. Respectful framing for AGA stakeholders

- Position MineAid as **supporting** AGA’s existing OHSE commitments—not replacing governance, but **giving the “H” a proper digital home**.  
- Tie the case to **national digitization** and **international norms** for **continuity of care** and **governed health data** (section 2.1)—*the operation should not be behind this curve*.  
- Acknowledge **investments already made** in safety and operations; propose **alignment** of health data with the same rigour.  
- **Name the contracting model openly**: clinical services may be contractor-delivered; **digital transformation** still requires **operator** and **clinical leadership** buy-in, clear **data ownership**, and **procurement** clarity. Framing it early avoids confusion and builds trust.  
- Offer a **phased path**: discovery → pilot site or pilot module set → scale—so risk and cost are controlled.  
- Invite **clinical, nursing, occupational health, IT, and safety** into the same conversation early to avoid “IT project” isolation.
